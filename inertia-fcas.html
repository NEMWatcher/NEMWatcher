📖 Plain English: What this chart is showing

The blue line is the total "spinning mass" of the NEM — physical inertia from generators locked to the grid. As coal plants retired, this fell dramatically. Each coal unit that closes takes hundreds of megawatt-seconds of inertia with it.

The orange line is what consumers pay every year to replace what that spinning mass provided for free. Frequency control ancillary services — 8 separate markets for fast and slow frequency response — have grown from $200M to $1.43B/year as inertia has fallen.

The purple dots are synchronous condensers — retired generator shafts kept spinning purely to provide inertia. Each costs $50–150M to install. We are literally paying to buy back, separately and expensively, what coal plants gave us as a free byproduct of generating electricity.

Why are there 8 markets?

Frequency can go too high (oversupply) or too low (undersupply). Each direction needs responses at 3 speeds: 6-second (arrest the swing), 60-second (stabilise), and 5-minute (restore to 50Hz). That's 6 markets. Plus two "regulation" services for continuous fine-tuning. Total: 8. Each clears separately. Each has exploded in cost as the grid lost its natural inertia buffer.

What does a synchronous condenser actually do?

Take a coal generator. Remove the boiler and steam system. Keep just the massive spinning shaft and generator connected to the grid. It consumes a small amount of power to keep spinning, produces zero electricity — but its rotational mass is locked to grid frequency. When frequency drops, physics automatically pushes current from the spinning mass into the grid in milliseconds. Faster than any computer can respond. This is what the grid lost when coal closed, and what we are now paying $50–150M per unit to replace.

What the scatter plot reveals

Each dot is a recorded frequency disturbance event. The horizontal axis is the inertia level at the time of the event. The vertical axis is how far frequency deviated from 50Hz. The pattern is unambiguous: as inertia falls (moving left), the same type of event causes a larger frequency swing. The red dots in the bottom-left corner are the dangerous ones — low inertia, extreme deviation. These are becoming more common and are reported in AEMO's quarterly frequency performance reports, which almost nobody reads.

Why doesn't this appear on your bill?

These costs are real and you pay them — but they're bundled into "network charges" and "market fees" as line items that appear on retailer invoices without explanation. The FCAS component alone has risen from roughly $2/MWh in 2017–18 to ~$14/MWh in 2023–24. For a household using 6,500kWh/year, that's gone from $13 to $91 per year — just for frequency services. A government dashboard showing "renewables are now the cheapest form of generation" is quoting the wholesale energy price. It is not quoting the total system cost per MWh delivered to your home.
