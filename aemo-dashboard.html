What does this mean?

The grid must stay very close to 50Hz at all times. Think of it like the "heartbeat" of the electricity network. When too many generators go offline suddenly (or demand spikes), frequency drops. When there's too much power, it rises.

⚠ Hidden cost: As intermittent renewables replace synchronous generators (coal, gas, hydro), the grid loses "inertia" — the physical spinning mass that naturally buffers frequency swings. This is replaced by expensive FCAS (Frequency Control Ancillary Services) contracts. Australians pay for this invisibly in their bills.

What does this mean?

The wholesale spot price is what generators get paid every 5 minutes. The price you see on government charts is a smooth monthly average — this hides the real story: extreme price spikes (up to $15,100/MWh cap) that occur when the wind drops and gas must fill the gap.

⚠ Hidden spin: Governments cite "record low renewable costs" using Levelised Cost of Energy (LCOE) — this ignores the system costs of intermittency: backup capacity, grid upgrades, storage, and frequency services.

What does this mean?

"Dispatchable" means power you can turn on when you need it. Coal, gas, and hydro can be ramped up on demand. Solar and wind cannot — they only produce when sun shines or wind blows. This figure shows what % of current generation you could actually control right now.

What is FCAS?

Frequency Control Ancillary Services. These are expensive contracts paid to generators to stand ready to inject or absorb power within seconds. As coal plants close and are replaced by wind/solar, FCAS costs have exploded because there is less natural grid inertia.

⚠ Why you don't hear about this: FCAS costs are bundled into network charges and not shown on household bills as a line item. Total NEM FCAS costs have risen from ~$200M/year to over $1.4B/year.

What is curtailment?

Sometimes solar and wind produce more power than the grid can use or transmit. Operators literally switch off (curtail) renewable generators — wasting the energy. You're still paying for the infrastructure that built them via your network charges, but the power is thrown away.

⚠ South Australia regularly curtails 15–30% of available solar. This hidden waste is rarely reported in "% renewable" achievements.

What "% Renewable" Actually Means SPIN DECODER
WHAT THEY SAY
"Australia hit 40% renewables!"
WHAT THEY MEAN
40% of total annual energy generated came from renewable sources — measured over a full year, including times when demand was low and weather was ideal.
WHAT THEY DON'T SAY
On a still winter evening at 6pm when demand peaks, renewables may supply 8–15%. The rest comes from coal and gas — which must be kept running 24/7 as backup, at full capital cost, even when only used part-time. This "capacity factor" problem is the central hidden cost of the transition.
The Duck Curve — Why "Too Much" Renewable Is Also A Problem
When solar floods the grid midday, prices crash (sometimes negative — generators pay to stay on). This destroys the business case for gas peakers that we need for evenings. So we pay them to stay alive via capacity mechanisms — another invisible cost. Then at sunset, demand surges and the grid must ramp gas/hydro extremely fast. This is called the duck curve, and it's getting more extreme every year as solar penetration grows.

Reserve margin explained

Reserve margin is the buffer of spare generating capacity above peak demand. Think of it like keeping extra water in a reservoir above the minimum level. When coal plants close faster than replacements are built, this buffer shrinks. Below 15%, AEMO considers reliability at risk — meaning rolling blackouts become more likely during heatwaves.

⚠ SA and Victoria have had reserve margin warnings in recent summers. This is rarely covered in mainstream energy reporting.

How to Interrogate This Dashboard

This dashboard is built to be challenged. Here's how:

1. Check the raw data
Every figure links to its AEMO source. Click any metric to see the underlying API call.
2. Review the calculations
The GitHub repository shows exactly how every derived number (like FCAS cost) is computed from raw data.
3. Propose corrections
Open a GitHub Issue or pull request. All changes are tracked publicly with full reasoning.
4. Fork it
MIT licensed. Run your own version. Change nothing. Or change everything. The data stays the data.
Editorial Policy

This dashboard does not oppose renewables. It opposes incomplete accounting. Every energy source has costs and benefits. This dashboard's goal is to show all of them — including the ones that are technically real but politically inconvenient.

We do not accept advertising. We do not accept funding from energy companies. The only agenda is accurate, complete data presented without spin from any direction — pro-coal or pro-wind.

If you believe a figure is wrong or misleading, the correct response is to open a GitHub issue with a source citation. Personal attacks or political framing will be closed without response.
